In 1948, neurologist William Grey Walter built two small robots at the Burden Neurological Institute in Bristol. Housed in transparent shells, Elmer and Elsie each ran on just two vacuum tubes — one for light-seeking, one for obstacle avoidance. Without any programming in the modern sense, they exhibited phototaxis, wandering, and spontaneous rest near their light source. In one experiment, a lamp was placed before a mirror: as the turtle approached, it detected its own reflected glow and entered a feedback loop of flickering and trembling. Walter wrote that, observed in an animal, this behaviour might well be taken as evidence of a rudimentary form of self-awareness.
